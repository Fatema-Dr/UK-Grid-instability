# Chapter 2: Literature Review

## 1. Introduction
The global energy landscape is currently navigating a profound shift, characterized by the decarbonization of power systems to mitigate climate change. This transition is fundamentally changing the operational dynamics of electrical grids, moving from centralized, fossil-fuel-based synchronous generation to decentralized, asynchronous renewable energy sources (RES).

### 1.1. The Global Energy Transition and Grid Stability
The growth of Variable Renewable Energy Sources (VRES) like Wind and Solar photovoltaic (PV) systems is rapidly increasing, as these are important to enhance energy security and reduce greenhouse-gas emissions. However, the inherent variability of VRES impacts grid stability, reliability and grid energy distribution (Che et al. 2025). Unlike with traditional synchronous generators that provide stable, dispatchable power, energy generation from variable renewable sources is dependent upon varying meteorological conditions. Che et al. (2025) describe this as introducing a "chaos" into the power system, where supply doesn’t meet demand patterns, complicating the fundamental supply-demand balance required for stable grid operation.

Zhao et al., (2024) have recognized that the increasing penetration of weather-dependent renewable energy sources (WD-RES) has raised legitimate concerns for functioning of electric power systems in abnormal weather events. Though there is a fabricated perception that VRES are unstable and thus create instability in the electric grid, research indicates that the intense nature of blackouts and susceptibility to extreme weather may be reduced in grids running on Weather dependent RES if they are operated with advanced functionality and advanced control strategies (Zhao et al. 2024).

### 1.2. Frequency Stability in Low-Inertia Power Systems
A critical consequence of displacing synchronous generators (SGs) with inverter-based resources (IBRs) like wind and solar is the reduction of system inertia. Tielens & Van Hertem (2016) defined inertia as the resistance of the system’s rotating mass to changes in frequency. In traditional systems, the kinetic energy stored in the rotors of synchronous generators provides an immediate, natural response to power imbalances, slowing the Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF).

As this mechanical inertia is replaced by static inverters, the system becomes "lighter" and more volatile. In low-inertia grids, frequency deviations occur more rapidly and deeply following a disturbance (Saleem et al., 2024). The reduced inertial buffer means that the time available for primary frequency response mechanisms to arrest a frequency drop is significantly curtailed. Consequently, there is a critical need for "synthetic" or "virtual" inertia control strategies that mimic the behaviour of synchronous machines using power electronics to maintain stability within permissible limits (Saleem et al., 2024; Tielens & Van Hertem, 2016).

Research indicates that low-inertia power systems exhibit heightened sensitivity to disturbances, where it can lead to a high Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF) and substantial frequency deviations (Saleem et al., 2024). The instability phenomenon is characterized by the fact that RoCoF does not vary smoothly but can shift abruptly, posing operational challenges in real-time system management (Amamra, 2025).

The risks are exemplified by the 9 August 2019 blackout in the UK. This event was triggered by a lightning strike that caused the near simultaneous loss of two large generators - Hornsea One and Little Barford, resulting in a frequency drop to 48.8 Hz and the automatic disconnection of approximately 1.1 million customers (Ofgem, 2019; Amamra, 2025). This incident highlighted that reduced system inertia, resulting from increased RES penetration, was a major contributing factor (Saleem et al., 2024).

### 1.3. The Great Britain Grid as a Critical Case Study
The power system of Great Britain (GB) serves as a premier case study for these challenges. The GB transmission system faces unique frequency control challenges because its renewable resources are non-uniformly distributed, and its lower system capacity makes it more sensitive to infeed losses compared to the larger continental European grid (Hong et al., 2021).
Historical analysis reveals that the GB system is experiencing an increasing number of excursions outside operational limits. Increased periods of renewable energy supply have coincided with decreasing levels of system inertia, thus making the system more susceptible to frequency related events (Homan & Brown, 2020).

## 2. Quantifying Grid Instability
To move from reactive protection to proactive alerting, the definition of instability must evolve from simple frequency deviation thresholds to dynamic, leading proxy variables.

### 2.1. Operational Definitions
Maintaining stability in the electric grid is accomplished by keeping the frequency of the electric grid in accordance with regulatory and operational constraints. The nominal frequency for the UK National Grid ESO is 50 Hz. Using research data, the operational limit has been established as 0.2 Hz (i.e. from 49.8 Hz to 50.2 Hz), which is a very important threshold for identifying an event of instability (Homan & Brown, 2020). Any deviation beyond this range is considered an "event" and requires some level of intervention.

The definition of stability continues to change with the development of Smart Grids. New frameworks like Decentralized Smart Grid Control (DSGC) are emerging, which link electricity pricing to grid frequency dynamics (Ucar, 2023).

Instability does not only occur when the frequency moves beyond physical limits, but also when there is a disparity between supply and demand. Data-driven models that utilize predictive analytics can help identify events of instability in early phases and facilitate economic-intervention mechanisms such as Demand-Side Management (DSM) prior to reaching the point of violating physical limits (Ucar, 2023).

### 2.2 Proxy Variables
Two primary metrics serve as proxies for frequency stability in low-inertia systems:

*   **Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF):** RoCoF is the instantaneous rate at which frequency changes following a disturbance (df/dt). It is identified as the most critical metric for low-inertia grids because high RoCoF values can trigger "Loss of Mains" (LoM) protection on distributed generators, causing them to trip offline and further exacerbate power imbalances (Amamra, 2025; Hong et al., 2021).

*   **Frequency Nadir:** Frequency Nadir indicates the lowest level attained by the system frequency before recovering. Accurate prediction of the minimum frequency level (nadir) is crucial, as it will trigger forced load shedding if the frequency drops below threshold values (i.e., 48.8 Hz in the U.K.).

Recent studies have proposed Frequency Nadir Index (FNI) that identifies the linear characteristics of inertia and reserve capacity in relation to the nadir, facilitating faster estimation (Son et al., 2024). Furthermore, online estimation of disturbance size is a prerequisite for accurate nadir prediction, allowing operators to deploy faster frequency control (Lekshmi et al., 2024).

*   **Market-Based Inertia Costs:** A novel, pre-event proxy for grid fragility is the cost of procuring inertia services. Forecasting system inertia service costs allows operators to quantify the "financial stress" of the grid, which correlates strongly with physical scarcity. Analysis of National Grid ESO data from 2017–2024 reveals a steady rise in costs with a sharp increase observed between 2020 and 2022, reflecting the growing reliance on flexible, inertia-providing ancillary services (Dey et al., 2025). High predicted inertia costs serve as a proxy for periods of critically low physical inertia, warning of potential instability before any physical disturbance occurs.

## 3. Data-Driven Forecasting Methodologies
The transition from physical modelling to data-driven forecasting is necessitated by the complexity and non-linearity of modern power systems.

### 3.1. Benchmarking Against Naive Persistence
To validate the efficacy of any advanced model, it must be benchmarked against a Naive Persistence model, which assumes that the future value will equal the current value (y_{t+1} = y_t). While simple, persistence is a difficult baseline to beat for very short-term horizons in power systems. However, in renewable forecasting contexts, advanced models like Random Forest and Gradient Boosting have been shown to significantly outperform baselines in terms of RMSE and Skill Score (Gaboitaolelwe et al., 2024), justifying their computational cost.

### 3.2 Machine Learning Models
*   **Classical Statistical Models (SARIMAX):**
    The SARIMAX (Seasonal AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average with eXogenous regressors) model remains a standard statistical baseline. While SARIMAX is widely used for its interpretability and ability to model seasonality (Ashtar et al., 2025), it struggles with high-frequency non-linearities. In a 2025 study on electricity demand, classical statistical models like SARIMAX were outperformed by deep learning models in capturing complex temporal dependencies (Ashtar et al., 2025).
    To address the limitations of statistical models, researchers are increasingly turning to Machine Learning (ML) and Deep Learning (DL) algorithms.

*   **Gradient Boosting Machines (LightGBM):**
    LightGBM (Light Gradient Boosting Machine) has emerged as a superior method for power grid frequency prediction due to its computational efficiency. For tasks involving tabular market data or high-frequency grid signals, LightGBM is often superior due to speed and accuracy. In forecasting system inertia service costs, LightGBM achieved the highest predictive accuracy, outperforming LSTM, Residual LSTM, and XGBoost (Dey et al., 2025). Furthermore, a LightGBM-based framework reduced the RMSE by 5.2% to 10.4% compared with benchmark methods in grid frequency prediction scenarios (Zhou et al., 2025).

*   **Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) Networks:**
    Deep learning architectures, specifically LSTM networks, are dominant in renewable energy forecasting. LSTMs are designed to capture long-term temporal dependencies in sequential data, which is crucial for forecasting complex waveforms in wind and solar generation (Mollasalehi & Farhadi, 2025). While LSTMs generally offer superior accuracy for complex signals, they can be computationally more intensive than tree-based models like LightGBM.

*   **Hybrid and Ensemble Modeling:**
    The literature increasingly points toward hybrid models to maximize performance. Ashtar et al. (2025) demonstrate that a SARIMAX-LSTM hybrid model can outperform standalone statistical models by combining the linear trend capture of SARIMAX with the non-linear residual modeling capabilities of LSTM. Additionally, Sequence-to-Sequence (Seq2Seq) deep learning models have achieved the highest accuracy for long-horizon forecasting in specific energy demand studies (Ashtar et al., 2025).

### 3.3. Data Integration and Feature Engineering
The integration of diverse datasets is critical. Studies on the Nigerian power system emphasize that integrating diverse datasets is necessary to accurately assess the impact of renewable penetration levels on stability indicators like voltage and frequency fluctuations (Ohiri et al., 2025).
Also, Raw weather data is insufficient for predicting stability; features must be engineered to expose the underlying physics.
*   **Ramp Rate Extraction (OpSDA):**
    Sudden changes in renewable output are the primary drivers of frequency deviation. To model this, the Optimized Swinging Door Algorithm (OpSDA) is introduced to detect wind power ramp events, allowing for the extraction of ramp features, such as the ramp rate (Zhang et al., 2024). These explicit ramp features allow the model to anticipate the "jolt" to the system, rather than just the absolute power level.

*   **Lag Features:**
    For models like LightGBM, which do not inherently process sequence order, lag features are critical. Including time-based features like hour or day of the week and historical lag features (e.g., lag_1, lag_2) has been shown to significantly improve forecasting accuracy by capturing temporal trends and autocorrelation (Dey et al., 2025). In inertia cost forecasting, 7 lag variables were generated to capture persistence, proving essential for model performance (Dey et al., 2025).

### 3.4. Critical Analysis of Forecasting Methodologies
A synthesis of the reviewed literature reveals a distinct divergence in modeling philosophies for grid stability. While deep learning architectures like LSTM and Sequence-to-Sequence models demonstrate superior accuracy in capturing long-term temporal dependencies in demand forecasting (Ashtar et al., 2025), they often suffer from high computational latency and opacity. In contrast, Gradient Boosting Machines (specifically LightGBM) have emerged in 2024–2025 literature as the optimal "operational" solution. Zhou et al. (2025) and Dey et al. (2025) independently confirm that LightGBM offers the best trade-off between predictive accuracy (lowest RMSE for inertia costs) and inference speed, which is critical for real-time fault detection.
Furthermore, a significant methodological limitation identified across the literature is the reliance on deterministic "point forecasts" (predicting a single value). As noted by Saleem et al. (2024), low-inertia grids are stochastic systems where the "tail risk" (the 1% chance of a blackout) is more operationally relevant than the average behavior. Most existing studies evaluate models using standard regression metrics (RMSE, MAE); however, these metrics fail to capture the severity of missing a rare stability event. Consequently, recent research advocates for the adoption of probabilistic evaluation metrics, such as Pinball Loss (Osone & Kodaira, 2025), to validate the reliability of prediction intervals rather than just the mean accuracy.

## 4. Alert Systems, Explainability (XAI), and Validation
Moving from raw predictions to a decision-support tool requires uncertainty quantification, model interpretability, and rigorous validation.

### 4.1. Probabilistic and Uncertainty-Aware Forecasting
Deterministic forecasts are insufficient for managing the risks associated with VRES. A stochastic approach that incorporates uncertainties in RES generation and load demand is advocated for effective stability assessment (Saleem et al., 2024).
To quantify this uncertainty, **Quantile Regression (QR)** is employed to estimate specific percentiles of the target variable. A 2025 study on the UK market showed that a QR model achieved high predictive accuracy and stability, providing reliable prediction intervals for volatile metrics (Osone & Kodaira, 2025). The performance of these probabilistic forecasts must be evaluated using the **Pinball Loss function** (or Winkler Score), which measures the calibration and sharpness of the predicted probability distributions, rather than standard RMSE (Osone & Kodaira, 2025).

### 4.2. Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) in Grid Operations
Black-box models like LSTM and LightGBM often face resistance from grid operators due to lack of transparency. To deploy AI in control rooms, operators must understand why a model predicts instability. To address this, **SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)** values have been successfully integrated into smart grid stability prediction (Ucar, 2023).
SHAP provides a unified measure of feature importance, allowing the system to explain why a specific alert was triggered. This interpretability is essential for building trust and facilitating the adoption of ML models by grid operators (Ucar, 2023).
*   **Identifying Drivers:** SHAP values allow researchers to identify key features and risk factors for frequency stability (Kruse et al., 2021). For example, SHAP analysis has identified solar ramps as critical to explain patterns in the Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF) (Kruse et al., 2021).
*   **Drift and Diffusion:** In analyzing grid frequency dynamics, SHAP analysis can distinguish between deterministic drivers (e.g., scheduled generation changes) and stochastic drivers (e.g., weather noise), identifying that total generation and load influence the drift, while calendar features seem critical for the diffusion (Drewnick et al., 2025).
*   **Model Validation:** For inertia forecasting, LightGBM models analyzed with SHAP reveal the importance of specific temporal features, validating that the model is learning realistic grid physics rather than spurious correlations (Dey et al., 2025).

### 4.3. Validation and Alert System Performance
Validating an alert system requires rigorous metrics beyond simple accuracy.Validating against historical extreme events, such as the August 9 blackout, serves as the ultimate stress test. The system's ability to predict such "tail events" is measured not just by accuracy, but by Precision (avoiding false alarms) and Recall (detecting all real threats) (Gayathri & Jena, 2025).

### 4.4. Developing Decision Support Systems
The literature supports the use of dashboards to convert predictive intelligence into actionable information. Machine learning outputs, such as forecasted inertia costs, can be used for strategic planning and market participation, implying the need for interfaces that present these forecasts to trading and control room teams (Dey et al., 2025).
Dashboards that integrate probabilistic forecasting enable decision-makers to weigh the potential costs and benefits of different actions under different scenarios, such as determining the optimal unit commitment or energy storage management strategies. Effectively presenting "Time to Alert" and instability probabilities allows for the deployment of reserves or demand response services before the system reaches its critical limits (Ucar, 2023).

### 4.5. The Research Gap
Despite the extensive body of work on grid stability, a critical gap remains in the integration of these disparate methodologies into a cohesive decision-support framework for the UK context. Existing studies typically focus on isolated components: Amamra (2025) quantifies inertia but does not build a predictive alert system; Dey et al. (2025) forecast costs but not physical stability metrics like RoCoF; and Zhou et al. (2025) focus on frequency prediction without fully integrating the explainability required for operator trust.
There is currently no unified framework that:
1.  Combines physics-informed feature engineering (e.g., OpSDA ramp detection) with market-based inertia proxies to predict instability.
2.  Benchmarks LightGBM against LSTM specifically for the UK's islanded grid constraints (low inertia, high wind penetration).
3.  Provides probabilistic "Time to Alert" warnings validated by SHAP values to ensure the "black box" is interpretable for control room operators.
This project addresses this gap by developing an end-to-end Data-Driven Alert System that bridges the divide between accurate deep learning forecasting and practical, risk-aware grid operations.

## 5. Conclusion
This literature review establishes that the decarbonization of the Great Britain electricity grid has precipitated a fundamental "Inertia Crisis," rendering the system increasingly susceptible to rapid Rate of Change of Frequency (RoCoF) events and deep frequency nadirs (Amamra, 2025; Hong et al., 2021). The 9 August 2019 blackout serves as a definitive case study, highlighting the inadequacy of traditional protection mechanisms in a grid dominated by asynchronous, inverter-based renewable generation.
The review of forecasting methodologies indicates a paradigm shift from linear statistical models (SARIMAX) to advanced machine learning techniques. While LSTM networks excel in capturing long-term sequential dependencies (Ashtar et al., 2025), the literature suggests that LightGBM offers a superior balance of accuracy and computational efficiency for high-frequency grid data (Dey et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2025). However, accuracy alone is insufficient; the stochastic nature of wind and solar generation necessitates a move from deterministic forecasting to probabilistic uncertainty quantification using Quantile Regression (Osone & Kodaira, 2025).
Furthermore, the "black box" nature of these advanced models remains a barrier to adoption in safety-critical control rooms. The integration of Explainable AI (XAI) via SHAP values has been identified as a mandatory requirement to provide operators with the "why" behind an alert (Ucar, 2023; Kruse et al., 2021).
Consequently, this project aims to fill the identified research gap by developing a holistic Data-Driven Alert System. By integrating physics-informed feature engineering (OpSDA), benchmarking LightGBM against LSTM, and employing SHAP for interpretability, this research will provide a robust, transparent, and probabilistic tool to forecast and mitigate grid instability in the UK's evolving low-carbon energy landscape.
